# Nostalgia Snack Archaeology — Defunct & Transformed Brands

Brands and companies that no longer exist in their original form. Some products survive under new ownership (sometimes manufactured elsewhere); some are completely gone. Cataloged here because the *nostalgia* often outlives the *product* — and the disappearance is itself a regional cultural data point.

## Inclusion criteria for archaeology

- **Truly defunct** (company gone, no successor): full entry
- **Defunct with brand acquired and reformulated** (Necco-style, Hostess-style during bankruptcy years): full entry
- **Defunct with brand acquired and moved overseas / out of region** (Pixy Stix → Mexico): full entry
- **Defunct and revived by descendants** (Switzer's, Mavrakos): documented in the *main survey*, with an "original-run" archaeological note here

## Missouri

### Sunline Inc. / Sunmark Corporation

```yaml
name: Sunline Inc. / Sunmark Corporation
category: candy company (powdered sugar candies)
products_originated:
  - Lik-M-Aid (1952)
  - Pixy Stix (1959)
  - SweeTarts (1963)
  - Sprees
  - Fun Dip (~1973, Lik-M-Aid evolution)
city: St. Louis, MO (Affton offices)
founded: 1952 (as Sunline Inc.)
defunct: 2006 (Nestlé closed STL operations)
current_status: products survive under Ferrara Candy Co. (Ferrero Group), manufactured in Mexico
ownership_chain: Sunline → Sunmark Corporation → Rowntree Mackintosh (1986, UK) → Nestlé (1988) → Ferrara Candy Co. / Ferrero (2018)
heritage_value: high — four iconic American candies from one STL company
```

The largest single archaeological finding for Missouri: four widely-recognized candies that most Americans don't know are St. Louis inventions. The Fruzola Company became Sunline, Inc., founded in 1952 by Menlo F. Smith to manufacture and market Lik-M-Aid nationwide. The whole arc started with J. Fish Smith noticing his kids were tearing open packets of fruit-flavored drink mix and eating the powder dry.

In 1959, the product was packaged in color-striped straws and introduced as Pixy Stix. Several years later, Lik-M-Aid was modified with a multi-compartment package containing two flavors and a candy stick used to dip the candy out of the package, thereby dubbed Fun Dip. SweeTarts came from compressing the Pixy Stix powder into a tablet; Sprees came from panning SweeTarts to get a sugar shell.

Sunline (and later Sunmark) was sold to the U.K.'s Rowntree Mackintosh, which was then bought out by Switzerland's Nestlé. As of 2006, Nestlé shut down the original Sunline/Sunmark offices still located in St. Louis. Pixy Stix is now produced in Mexico under Ferrara Candy Co.

This is a textbook AIA case (acquire / integrate / abandon) — the products live on, but the company, the jobs, and the manufacturing presence are all gone from STL.

### Switzer's Licorice — original 1888-1998 run

```yaml
name: Switzer Licorice Co. (original entity)
category: candy (licorice)
city: St. Louis, MO (Eads Bridge area, then Webster Groves move 1968)
founded: 1888
defunct: 1998 (Hershey shut down STL plant after 1996 acquisition)
current_status: REVIVED 2004-2005 by family descendants — see main survey
ownership_chain: Murphy/Switzer family → Beatrice Foods (1966) → Leaf Industries → Hershey (1996, killed)
heritage_value: high — 110-year original run, factory was riverfront landmark
```

Original-run details preserved here for archaeological completeness. The full revived brand entry lives in the main survey.

The original factory by the Eads Bridge had ghost-sign cultural status — the building had long since been abandoned by Switzer Candy Company in the time of my youth, but its cast iron green façade stood almost beckoning, with overlapping painted signs through the years, the most recent of which was a picture of a candy licorice box reading "Home of Switzer's Old Fashioned Licorice." The original factory was torn down in 2007.

The death pattern (acquired by big confectioner, killed because it competes with their existing product) is recurring: Hershey made Twizzlers Licorice, so Switzer Licorice was soon put out of production.

### Mavrakos Candy Company — original 1913-1984 run

```yaml
name: Mavrakos Candy Company (original entity)
category: chocolate / confections
city: St. Louis, MO
founded: 1913 (incorporated); 1909 earlier as Opal Candy Company
defunct: 1984 (sold to Archibald Candy)
current_status: REVIVED 2009 — see main survey
ownership_chain: John L. Mavrakos / Madeline Mavrakos → Tom Wotka (last owner) → Archibald Candy (1984, killed) → Chocolate Chocolate Chocolate Co. (2009 revival, recipe book)
heritage_value: high — 16 retail stores at peak, 1959 Copper Kettle award winner
```

Original-run archaeological note. Full revived brand entry in main survey. Mavrakos peaked in the 1950s with 16 retail stores in the St. Louis area, a popular catalog and a state-of-the-art manufacturing facility.

The Mavrakos Candy Store at 4949 Delmar Boulevard (built 1927) was demolished in 1983, the year before the company itself ended.

### Old Vienna Snack Food Company — original 1936-1996 run

```yaml
name: Old Vienna Snack Food Company (original entity)
category: chips
city: St. Louis, MO
founded: 1936 (Louis Kaufman)
defunct: 1996
current_status: REVIVED 1996 by former employees — see main survey
ownership_chain: Louis Kaufman → multiple changes of hands → folded 1996 → former employees revived same year
heritage_value: medium — Red Hot Riplets product was introduced late 1970s, mid-original-run
```

Original-run note. The revival was unusually fast — the company folded and was resurrected by former employees in the *same year* (1996). Continuity of recipe is therefore stronger than typical revivals (Switzer's was dormant ~6-7 years before revival; Mavrakos was dormant 25 years).

## Pattern observations after Missouri

### Death-by-acquisition pattern

Three of four Missouri archaeology entries follow the same script:
1. Local family-run candy company achieves regional dominance
2. Acquired by larger conglomerate (Beatrice, Hershey, Archibald, Nestlé)
3. Brand killed because it competes with parent's existing line, OR factory closed for cost reasons
4. Original recipes / equipment / staff dispersed

Worth tracking this rate across other states — hypothesis is that the Midwest got hit harder than coastal regions due to consolidation patterns in mid-20th-century food manufacturing.

### Revival-by-descendants pattern

Switzer's and Mavrakos both feature literal family / community descendants reviving the brand:
- **Switzer's:** Twin grandsons of founder Frederick Switzer (Michael and Joe Switzer)
- **Mavrakos:** Recipe book passed to neighbor (Dan Abel of CCC, who grew up on the same street as last owner Tom Wotka)
- **Old Vienna:** Former employees themselves

This is a Midwest cultural pattern worth flagging. None of the regional NE / SE / West revivals I've documented so far follow this exact template.

### Building-as-relic

The Switzer factory by Eads Bridge → demolished 2007. The Mavrakos store on Delmar → demolished 1983. The original 1922 Chase Candy factory in St. Joseph still stands but houses an antique mall now. The defunct buildings *were* the heritage in a way the new factories aren't — worth photographing or visiting on your next STL/SJ adventure if these are still accessible.
