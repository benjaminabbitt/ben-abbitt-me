# Restaurants

Find small chains in the **goldilocks zone**: big enough to prove the food's worth traveling for, small enough not to be hollowed out by the corporate machine.

**The archetype:** Gioia's Deli, St. Louis. 1–2 locations. James Beard American Classic. Hot salami sandwich, same recipe since 1918. Big enough to have a reputation. Small enough that the food is still the point. That's the target in every city.

**The zone:** ~2–35 locations, concentrated in one metro, still owned by the founding family or chef. Three conditions, all required:

1. **Demand pulled them, capital didn't push them.** Location two answered a line out the door, not a pitch deck.
2. **The recipe is still the recipe.** The owner could plausibly be in the kitchen. Sourcing decisions are made by someone who cares more about the food than the P&L.
3. **There's a *thing* you go for.** Signature dish, specific technique, regional style at the top of its class. "Solid" doesn't qualify. You should know what to order before you walk in.

**Why it's hard to find.** Yelp and Google surface independents and nationals — small chains fall in the gap (and Google's chain ratings skew ~0.5 stars high vs. Yelp). "Best of" listicles confuse cultural significance with food quality; an institution with mediocre food is still mediocre food. No consumer tool filters by location count. The closest thing is the [Georgia Tech Chainness Map](https://friendlycities-gatech.github.io/chainness/), and its data is from 2021.

**The method.** [finding-small-chains.md](finding-small-chains.md) — a 5-step pre-trip workflow, a calibrated LLM prompt, and worked examples (St. Louis, Texas, Southwest, an Austin test run).

**Open questions:**
- Is 35 the right ceiling, or does it depend on ownership? Boiling Crab at ~30 still feels intact; some 8-location chains already feel hollow.
- Earliest review-language signal that a chain has tipped from earned growth to capital-funded growth? Portion shrinkage and recipe changes are lagging indicators.
- The method depends on active food media and engaged city subreddits — smaller metros are under-covered, and may be where the best undiscovered chains actually live.
