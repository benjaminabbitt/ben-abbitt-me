# The Freddy

## What it is

A pounded, fennel-laced Italian sausage patty (the same sausage Chicago pizza joints use), griddled or grilled into an oblong patty, then laid on a sturdy French roll and smothered in marinara, sautéed green bell peppers, and melted mozzarella. Not a hot Italian sausage link split lengthwise (that's a different Chicago sandwich). The patty is the defining structural element. A close cousin to Upper Peninsula cudighi (also a fennel-sausage-patty sandwich), but the Freddy uses a French roll rather than cudighi's bread choice and pairs sweet peppers and mozzarella in an "Italian pico" configuration that's the Beverly signature.

## Origin

Benito "Benny" Russo invented the Freddy at Chuck's Pizza in Beverly in the early 1970s and named it after his son Freddy. When Chuck's was sold in the late 1970s, Benny opened Calabria Imports on Western Avenue in Blue Island, taking the Freddy with him. Calabria Imports moved to its current 103rd Street location in Beverly in August 2003; the Blue Island shop was renamed Stefanelli & Sons by the founders who stayed behind, and they kept the Freddy too. Both Beverly locations (Calabria Imports and Chuck's Pizza) still serve the original lineage.

## Why it's contained

- **Geographic containment**: Sandwich Tribunal calls the Freddy "a nearly-extinct sandwich sold at Calabria Imports in Beverly and a few other Italian delis scattered throughout the south side and the south suburbs." Diningchicago.com: "I don't think I've seen it north of 99th Street." Its presence drops off above the Stevenson Expressway and is essentially nonexistent outside the Chicagoland south/southwest corridor.
- **Name-recognition failure**: Order a "Freddy" anywhere outside Beverly/Morgan Park/Blue Island/the south suburbs and you'll get a blank stare. The dish is unknown above Cermak.
- **No national chain adoption**: No national franchise carries it. Regional pizza chains Beggars Pizza and Barraco's both offer it, but those are Chicagoland-only chains.
- **Active local culture**: Multi-vendor presence (Chuck's, Calabria, Stefanelli & Sons, Beggars, Barraco's, Fabulous Freddie's on 31st), plus aliases at neighboring shops (Big Nicky, Durbin Special, Sandro's Special) demonstrating that the *form* is locked enough to spawn parallel names. Featured on Jeff Mauro's *Sandwich King* (Food Network) and *Chicago's Best*.

**Containment mechanism**: Immigrant-community-locked (Italian-American Far Southwest Side) plus a tight family-and-shop lineage (Chuck's → Calabria → Stefanelli & Sons → regional Italian-American chains).

The Russo lineage parallels the Ricobene lineage (1976) for breaded steak: a single first-generation Italian-American shop on the south/southwest side spawning a multi-vendor sandwich tradition. The cudighi cross-reference (already a Great Lakes entry under UP Marquette) is worth a one-line mention since both are fennel-sausage-patty sandwiches from Italian-American immigrant communities; the divergent geographies are a nice illustration of how the same source ingredient produces two regionally-distinct sandwich traditions.
