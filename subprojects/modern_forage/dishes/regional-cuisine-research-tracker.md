- ✅ **Pizza Pot Pie — Chicago Pizza and Oven Grinder Co., Lincoln Park (1972)** (validated 2026-05-04, 6+ named sources). **PROMOTED**. Single-restaurant dish since 1972: triple-raised Sicilian dough draped over a deep ceramic bowl filled with plum-tomato sauce, button mushrooms, mozzarella, and Boston-butt sausage; baked upside-down so the dough forms a domed lid; flipped tableside to reveal the pot pie. Attorney Albert H. Beaver developed the recipe in 1972 after a fire destroyed the previous building at 2121 N Clark St. Recipe unchanged in 50+ years. Never franchised, never licensed, never expanded. Marc Murphy: "instantly deserving of *The Best Thing I Ever Ate*"; Rachael Ray's *Tasty Travels* filmed there. Sources: Wikipedia, The Infatuation, Roadfood, Food Network "Pizza Pot Pie" video, CPOG official, Chicago Tribune dining coverage, CBS Chicago. **Promotion rationale**: the dish is the restaurant — locked to one room in Lincoln Park for half a century, structurally analogous to other "operational-routine-locked" entries. Surfaced via /r/chicagofood (Myviewpoint62). Staged as `dishes/pizza-pot-pie.md` and now integrated into the canonical survey.

- ✅ **Atomic Cake — Chicago South Side (1950s)** (validated 2026-05-04, 7+ named sources). **PROMOTED**. Three-layer celebration cake with fixed composition (banana / chocolate / yellow cake stack with custard, glaze, fresh fruit, hot fudge, whipped cream icing). Created in the 1950s by George Kremm; origin contested between Calumet Bakery (South Deering) and Liberty Bakery (Roseland). Now sold at nearly every South and Southwest Side bakery; bestselling item at Weber's, Calumet (Lansing), Wolf's. **Critical containment lever**: never sold by the slice — whole cakes only — which kills casual diffusion in a way that single-serving formats don't. Hawksmoor Chicago's Carla Henriques recently introduced a thirteen-layer atomic-cake sundae as the first downtown reinterpretation in 70 years. Sources: Block Club Chicago (2025 70th-anniversary longform), WTTW Chicago (2022), Atlas Obscura / Gastro Obscura, Tasting Table, Chowhound, Mashed, Daily Meal, Secret Chicago. **Promotion rationale**: structurally distinct containment lever (commitment-purchase-locked) that the survey hadn't previously documented; potentially seeds a fourth containment-mechanism category (alongside immigrant-community-locked, apparatus-locked, operational-routine-locked). Surfaced via /r/chicagofood (LemonBerryCake). Staged as `dishes/atomic-cake.md` and now integrated.

- ✅ **Taffy Grapes — Chicago South Side (~2011)** (validated 2026-05-04, 3+ named sources plus geographic-containment evidence). **PROMOTED**. Whole green grapes dipped in white almond bark, rolled in crushed peanuts, sold by the tub at corner stores, chicken shacks, fish shops, barbershops, and nail salons across Chicago's South and West sides. Emerged informally in Black food culture in the early 2010s; The Takeout's 2017 piece traces the form to ~2011. Tamara Brown's Nadia's Gourmet Grapes is the brand-name canonical version; flavor variants (peanut, Oreo, graham cracker, seasonal). **Containment notes**: under-covered by national food media — the lack of formal coverage is itself the containment evidence; distribution is hyper-local Black retail networks that don't export. Cross-references mild sauce and Chicago hot links as the same retail-network pattern. Surfaced via /r/chicagofood (bobcats2019). Staged as `dishes/taffy-grapes.md` and now integrated.

- ✅ **Walker Bros Apple Pancake — North Shore, Chicago (since 1960)** (validated 2026-05-04, 4+ sources). **PROMOTED**. 12-14" oven-baked apple pancake with Sinkiang cinnamon-sugar glaze. Walker Bros opened 1960 (Walter Walker, Raymond Lindemann); seven North Shore locations. **Containment subtlety**: the recipe descends from the Original Pancake House franchise (Portland, OR, 1953), so the dish has *two* legitimate homes (Portland and Chicago North Shore) but no third leg. By the "two places, no national attention" reading of containment, this qualifies — neither Original Pancake House nor Walker Bros has gone national, and the Walker Bros version with its Sinkiang glaze and *Ordinary People* (1980) cultural anchor is locally distinct. The previous treatment of Walker Bros as "chain-locked, fails origin test" was over-strict — what matters is national attention, not number of locations.

- ✅ **Flaming Saganaki — Chicago Greektown (1968)** (validated 2026-05-04, 5+ sources, INCLUDED WITH CAVEAT). **PROMOTED with containment caveat**. Tableside-flambéed kasseri/kefalograviera with brandy and lemon. Invented at The Parthenon (Chris Liakouras, 1968); competing claim from Petros Kogiones at Diana's (1964). Doesn't exist in Greece — flat saganaki does, but the flame and the *Opa!* are Chicago additions. **Containment caveat**: dish has spread to Greek restaurants nationwide and fails the strict geographic-containment test. Included as a historical diversion in the author's estimation: the Chicago invention story is well-documented, and the *practice-locked-but-dish-traveled* shape is structurally distinct from the rest of the survey (the city is locked to the *practice* — flame, chant, theater — even as the *dish* travels). Marked in-line with an explicit caveat blockquote so future readers understand it's marginalia. Pattern: Greek-American adaptation, Chicago Greektown immigrant restaurateur scene.

- ✅ **St. Louis-Style Pizza — St. Louis, MO** (validated 2026-05-04, 6+ sources). **PROMOTED**. Cracker-thin crust, sweetened tomato sauce, and **Provel** (cheddar/Swiss/provolone processed blend with low melting point) cut in 4-inch squares ("party cut"). Provel was developed in St. Louis in the 1940s (Costa Grocery + Hoffman Dairy collaboration); Imo's Pizza (1964, ~95 locations) is the dominant purveyor. **Containment**: the cheese itself is St. Louis-only at retail scale; cracker-crust + Provel + sweetened sauce + 4-inch-square combination doesn't exist elsewhere. Divisive even locally; loathed and beloved in roughly equal measure. Sources: Wikipedia, Eater STL, Sauce Magazine, Atlas Obscura, Riverfront Times, STL Post-Dispatch. **Cross-listed** with the regional-condiment-containment pattern through Provel. Was on the Phase 6 STL deferred list; finally promoted.

- ✅ **Snoots — St. Louis, MO** (validated 2026-05-04, 6+ sources). **PROMOTED**. Smoked pig snouts, served on white bread in a paper basket with sweet tomato BBQ sauce. Texture is part pork rind, part cracklin', part rib tip. Originated in St. Louis Black BBQ tradition during the Great Migration. Canonical: C&K Barbecue (1101 Tucker, 1963), Smoki O's, Roper's Ribs, Super Smoker's. **Containment near-total**: dish exists almost nowhere outside STL, no pork-snout supply chain elsewhere. Eddy Roper Jr.: "like a pork rind, but with a ton of meat on it." Sources: KSDK, Sauce Magazine, Riverfront Times, Feast, Eater STL, The Bittman Project, STL Post-Dispatch. **Cross-listed** with Chicago aquarium-smoked tip-and-link as parallel Black-BBQ regional signatures (cheap-cut byproduct elevated into a city-defining preparation). Was on the Phase 6 STL deferred list; finally promoted.

### Cross-cutting note: containment-mechanism categories

The 2026-05-04 reconciliation surfaces a possible meta-pattern worth a future post. The survey now contains entries locked in by at least four distinct mechanisms:

1. **Immigrant-community-locked** — most entries (Greek diner empire, Chinese-American adaptations, Cornish mining corridor, German-Russian Great Plains).
2. **Apparatus-locked** — Chicago aquarium smoker; Provel cheese; Old Vienna's Red Hot Riplet seasoning machinery.
3. **Operational-routine-locked** — Pizza Pot Pie at CPOG (the dough-and-bowl flip routine); Hot Brown service at Brown Hotel.
4. **Commitment-purchase-locked** — Atomic Cake (whole cakes only, no single-serving entry point); Pittsburgh's Cookie Table (you have to be invited to a wedding to encounter it at scale).

### Earlier promotions (2026-05-04, original wave — see below)

- ✅ **Akutagawa — Chicago's Japanese Breakfast** (validated 2026-05-04, 7+ sources). **PROMOTED**. Wok scramble of chopped hamburger meat (sometimes sausage or chicken), diced onions, green peppers, bean sprouts, and beaten eggs, served over white rice with brown gravy poured on top. Often comes with toast. Created in the late 1960s at Hamburger King (3435 N. Sheffield Ave, Wrigleyville) by owner Tom Yamauchi for a regular customer named George Akutagawa, after whom the dish is named. Emerged from the post-WWII Japanese-American community that resettled in Lakeview/Wrigleyville after West Coast internment. Monica Eng characterizes it as a "quiet attempt to assert their culture during a time when they were told not to." Often described as "reverse yoshoku." Currently served at Rice'N Bread (3435 N. Sheffield Ave, the original Hamburger King space, Korean ownership preserved the recipe), Fullerton Restaurant (off-menu), and Susie's. Hyper-local to Chicago's Lakeview/Wrigleyville orbit. Sources: *Made in Chicago: Stories Behind 30 Great Hometown Bites* (Monica Eng & David Hammond, U of Illinois Press 2023); Forest Park Review (April 2023, "Hometown bites of foods you've probably never tried"); Windy City Times (Sept 2023, Eng/Hammond interview); The Infatuation (Rice'N Bread review); LTHForum thread; Adam Witt / Omnivorous (recipe + YouTube documentary "Resurrecting Chicago's Forgotten Japanese Breakfast"); Chicago Style Eats YouTube ep 0101. **Promote to full entry; would be the second confirmed entry in the Japanese/Korean-American adaptation pattern alongside Seattle teriyaki.**

- ✅ **Hot Link Sandwich — Chicago South Side** (validated 2026-05-04, 5+ sources). **PROMOTED** (Great Lakes blog post folds it into a richer "Aquarium-Smoked Rib Tips & Hot Links" entry that leads with the apparatus rather than the sandwich; canonical doc has it as "Chicago Hot Link / Tip-and-Link"). Coarsely-ground spiced pork (or pork/beef) sausage seasoned with sage, cayenne, fennel, pepper. Smoked in an aquarium smoker (a glass-and-steel indoor smoker designed for Chicago fire code) then often fried, served on two slices of plain white bread with thin sweet-tangy mild sauce poured over the top. Eaten with a fork as often as by hand. Common variant: sliced links combined with rib tips in a "tip & link" combo. End-cuts (crispy ends from the smoker) are prized. Distinct from Texas Elgin-style red links (less spicy, fresh rather than cured) and from California chili-and-bun versions. Direct artifact of the Second Great Migration (1940s-60s), when Southern-born Black pitmasters from Mississippi, Arkansas, Tennessee settled the South and West Sides under restrictive covenants and opened storefront BBQ operations. Canonical: Lem's Bar-B-Q (311 E 75th St, founded 1954 by the Lemons brothers, **2025 James Beard America's Classic**); Honey 1 BBQ (Bronzeville, pitmaster Robert Adams Sr.); Uncle John's BBQ (founded by Mack Sevier); Barbara Ann's Bar-B-Que; Leon's Bar-B-Q (1940). Sources: Saveur ("Welcome to America's Unsung Barbecue City: Chicago"); Sandwich Tribunal (2019, "Sausage Sandwiches"); Adrian Miller (*Black Smoke* author, "Chicago BBQ Research Trip: Southside Edition"); WTTW (2025, Lem's JBF America's Classics coverage); SFA Lem's Bar-B-Q oral history. **Promote to full entry; cross-link heavily to Chicago Mild Sauce and Maxwell Street Polish (sibling Chicago sausage-on-bread tradition from a different lineage).**

- ✅ **Beef Roll — Suburban & Rural Northern Illinois** (validated 2026-05-04, 6+ sources). **PROMOTED**.

  Italian beef filling and mozzarella baked inside pizza dough (Stromboli-style), served with au jus on the side. Hyper-regional to northern/central Illinois OUTSIDE Chicago city, with the Illinois Valley (LaSalle / Peru / Ottawa corridor) appearing to be the heartland. Dominant menu name is "Beef Roll" (variants: "Italian Beef Roll," "Stuffed Beef Roll," "Beef Marchelloni," "What's Your Beef? Roll," "Beef Pocket"). The "beef burrito" name appears to be informal/regional rather than menu-language. Confirmed at: Pizzas By Marchelloni (Peru, Ottawa, Mendota, Joliet — signature item, sizes 7"/12"/16"); Lou's LaGrotto (Peru); Sam's Pizza (Ottawa, "over 50 years"); Sam's Pizza (Marseilles); Lil Bella's Pizzeria (LaSalle); Linda's Pizza Westside (Joliet); Pizza For U (Joliet/Shorewood/Channahon/Manhattan, "Beef Pocket" variant); Aurelio's Pizza (Joliet, Beef Roll $26.99 + Mini Beef Roll w/ fries); Nonno's Pizza (Berwyn); Salerno's Pizza (multiple IL locations); Pizza Pro's (DeKalb); Zanas Wood Fire Pizza (DeKalb); Enzo's Pizza. Joliet Patch editor John Ferak (2017) explicitly compared it to Wisconsin pasties and Nebraska runzas as a regional specialty unfamiliar outside its zone. No documented inventor; concentration in LaSalle County (a historically heavy Italian-immigrant coal/zinc-mining region) strongly suggests mid-20th-century Italian-American pizzeria origin there. **Critical containment signature**: the dish has dozens of menu listings but no Wikipedia entry, no food-historian writeup, no NYT piece, no Serious Eats coverage, no Eater Chicago feature, no LTHForum thread, no documented inventor or etymology. Broad local presence plus total media invisibility is itself the strongest possible containment signal in this survey. Sources: Joliet Patch ("Beef Rolls: Joliet Patch editor asks who's your favorite"); TripAdvisor ("Beef Roll - Reinvented Italian Beef" review of Marchelloni Peru); Nonno's Pizza menu (Berwyn); Salerno's menu; Marchelloni Peru menu; Lou's LaGrotto menu; multiple additional pizzeria menus across 8+ northern IL towns. **Promote to full entry; place in the Chicago section with explicit framing that it's the suburban/Illinois-Valley counterpoint to city Chicago's Italian beef.**

### Promoted to main doc (2026-05-04, second wave)

- ✅ **Breaded Steak Sandwich — Chicago South Side / Bridgeport** (validated 2026-05-04, 5+ sources plus television and podcast coverage). **PROMOTED**. Pounded-thin breaded beef cutlet, deep-fried, on a Turano-style Italian roll with marinara and mozzarella; hot giardiniera and sweet peppers as iconic add-ons. Created in 1976 at Ricobene's (252 W. 26th St, Bridgeport) by the second-generation Ricobene brothers (Sam, Frank, Russell). The 1976 invention claim is consensus across DNAinfo, South Side Weekly, Roadfood, and Sandwich Tribunal. Multi-shop diffusion (Fabulous Freddie's, Ferro's, Nonna Soluri's, Kathy De's, Gio's, Frangella, Rubino's, Amici, Stunod's, Tony's Italian Beef) clears the single-restaurant exclusion. Distinct from East Coast cutlet sandwiches via four elements: beef instead of veal/chicken, giardiniera as defining condiment, Turano Italian roll, dunked-in-marinara service. Sandwich Tribunal codified it in its "Chicago Sandwich Canon" (2023). Anthony Bourdain on *Parts Unknown*, USA Today's Ted Berg ("best sandwich in the world," 2015), Andrew Zimmern's *Spilled Milk* podcast. Notably absent from Eater Chicago "best sandwich" lists, which reinforces containment.

- ✅ **Beer Nuggets — DeKalb, IL / Northern Illinois** (validated 2026-05-04, 9+ sources). **PROMOTED**. The user originally remembered this as "beef nuggets / dough nuggets"; research confirmed the dish is **beer nuggets** (also dough nuggets, pizza nuggets, pizza butts), deep-fried unfilled chunks of pizza dough served with cheese sauce or marinara, often tossed in garlic-butter parmesan. **Distinct from pizza puffs** (Iltaco's filled flour-tortilla pocket, already documented). Origin: late 1970s in DeKalb at Sgt. Pepper's and J.P. Hannagan's, refined and popularized at Pizza Villa under Larry Finn. Food-fight invention legend documented in the Northern Star (NIU student paper). Migrated south into Chicago via the Rosati's chain (official menu lists "Dough Nuggets" with marinara only) and city pizzerias like the now-closed Nick's Pizza & Pub on Montrose. Sources: The Takeout, DeKalb County Online, Northern Star, Daily Chronicle / Shaw Local, WGN-TV Lunchbreak, Rosati's official menu, PMQ Pizza Magazine, WSIU Under Rocks podcast, Mashed.

### Cross-cutting note: Northern Illinois sub-region

### Promoted to main doc (2026-05-04, fifth wave)

- ✅ **Chicago Jerk Chicken Egg Roll** (validated 2026-05-04, 7+ named sources). **PROMOTED**. Hypothesis correction: defining filling is jerk chicken; cheese is common but not defining. Origin documented: sisters Ernesta "Dinkey" Berry/Lowery and Lekia Lowery opened L&B Soul Kitchen in Bellwood 2012; in 2015 they put jerk chicken into an egg roll because soul-food sales lagged behind their jerk chicken. Spawned Tastee Rolls (Bellwood + 79th St); near-ubiquitous on Black-owned and non-Chinese storefronts within ~5 years. Per Chicago Tribune's Nick Kindelsperger, explicitly Black restaurateurs adapting the Chinese-American form, not extending it. **Containment is partial, not absolute**: jerk-chicken egg rolls also exist at scattered Caribbean and soul-food spots in Atlanta, DC, Detroit, Yeadon PA. What's Chicago-specific: density, Black-owned West/South Side storefront-and-food-truck ecosystem, 12-inch jumbo format, long flavor-variant menus (jerk + Philly + Italian beef + gyro + **Obama** on one board). The Obama variant — a savory steak-and-cheese egg roll named after Chicago's most famous South Side resident — is at Tastee Rolls and Jay's per first-person diaspora signal. Sources: Chicago Tribune (Kindelsperger), Wednesday Journal/OakPark, Axios Chicago, ABC7, WGN-TV, Chi BBQ King, Tastee Rolls official. Promoted to canonical Chicago section, Great Lakes blog post, and Chinese-American Adaptations thematic post (as a "second-wave footnote" rather than a new entry, since this isn't Chinese-American, but a Black-Chicago adaptation of the Chinese-American form). **Two-wave Chicago illustration of the survey's thesis**: same wrapper (egg roll), same city, Cantonese-American mid-century adaptation followed by Black-Chicago Caribbean-influenced 2015 adaptation, locked in different neighborhood ecosystems that don't overlap. Cross-cutting note: this parallels and slightly post-dates the c. 2014 Chicago jerk-taco wave; both signal a broader Black-Chicago Caribbean-influenced food culture emerging in the 2010s. Worth tracking as a potential future cross-cutting essay if more entries surface from this wave.

### Promoted to main doc (2026-05-04, fourth wave)

- ✅ **Chicago-Style Egg Roll (Peanut Butter in the Filling)** (validated 2026-05-04, 6+ named sources). **PROMOTED**. Hypothesis correction: the peanut butter is in the filling (binding the pork, shrimp, cabbage), not in a dipping sauce. Standard hot mustard and orange duck sauce are still served alongside in the conventional way. Origin per Monica Eng's Tribune reporting: David Leong (Cantonese immigrant, WWII D-Day vet) picked up the peanut-butter trick at a Philadelphia restaurant in 1945 after his Army discharge; the technique migrated to Chicago and stuck as the default Cantonese-American house style. Two decades later Leong reinvented in Missouri and created Springfield-style cashew chicken (already a survey entry). **This creates a David Leong throughline tying two survey entries together** — the same originating figure for both Springfield cashew chicken (1963) and the Chicago peanut-butter egg roll convention (mid-century default). Cross-references added in both directions in the canonical doc and the relevant blog posts. Containment confirmed: Detroit explicitly contrasted ("same Chinese-American food, no peanut butter"); Twin Cities, St. Louis, Cleveland, coasts have no documented version. The technique itself isn't Chicago-invented (Philadelphia 1945 is earliest known) but the convention as default house style is Chicago-localized. Sources: The Takeout, Chicago Magazine ("Iconic Eats" Orange Garden 2021), Axios Chicago (best egg rolls 2022), LTHForum thread, Chi BBQ King blog, Chicago Tribune (Eng). Canonical purveyors: Orange Garden (1942 W Irving Park, opened 1926, Chicago's oldest continuously operating Chinese restaurant); Hunan Egg Roll King (4204 N Broadway). Most older Chicago Cantonese-American restaurants serve a version. Promoted to canonical Chicago section, Great Lakes blog post, and Chinese-American Adaptations thematic post (as 7th entry, with the David Leong throughline made explicit in "The pattern" closing).

### Promoted to main doc (2026-05-04, third wave)

- ✅ **Chicago Creamy Garlic Dressing** (validated 2026-05-04, 7+ named sources). **PROMOTED**. Mayo-and-sometimes-sour-cream creamy garlic salad dressing built on raw or powdered garlic, lemon juice or vinegar, often with buttermilk, yogurt, Worcestershire, hot sauce, or grated Parmesan. Created by Greek immigrant Peter Alexander at Alexander's Steak House on 63rd St; Conrad Hilton Jr. discovered it in 1950 and shipped monthly cases to himself and Elizabeth Taylor. Chicago Tribune food editor Ruth Ellen Church (Mary Meade column) drove citywide adoption starting 1964. Spread across three Chicago restaurant scenes: Greek steakhouses (Alexander's, The Branding Iron, Mark III, Berghoff), Italian-American pizzerias (Aurelio's, Pequod's, Vito & Nick's), and old-line carryouts and pubs (Candlelite since 1950, Fox's, Ignotz, Carson's Ribs, Void as homage). Chicago Pizza & Oven Grinder Co. ships sour-cream-version jars nationwide; Marie's Creamy Italian Garlic at Mariano's is the closest current supermarket proxy. Containment confirmed against East Coast: NYC, NJ, Boston Italian-American restaurants serve oil-and-vinegar instead. Diaspora signal documented in Chicago Tribune ("foods I miss," 2014) and a long-running LTHForum recipe-hunt thread. Sources: Block Club Chicago (Feb 2026 longform); two LTHForum threads; Chicago Tribune (Mary Meade columns 1964 onward); Chicago Pizza & Oven Grinder Co. official; Aurelio's menu; Marie's at Mariano's; Vanished Chicago Facebook recipe-hunt thread. **Important framing nuance**: not strictly an Italian-American tradition. Origin is Greek-American Chicago steakhouse; the dressing spread across Italian-American pizzerias, Greek steakhouses, and Chicago carryouts alike. The survey entry frames it as "Chicago creamy garlic" broadly, with cross-references to both Grocery Store Regionalism and the Greek Diner Empire pattern (where it's noted as an extension of the template into the steakhouse scene rather than a 7th hot-dog-counter entry).

### Key Methodology Learnings
1. State-level sweeps beat city-by-city tables — **but miss items in food-dense cities**
2. Diaspora nostalgia ("I moved from X and nobody knows Y") is the #1 lead generator
3. Community cookbooks are for corroboration, not cold discovery
4. Sub-75k towns produce the best discoveries (Rock Springs 23k, Corinth 15k, Freeman 1,300, Hudson 450)
5. Immigrant adaptation patterns are the most distinctive finding — same structure across Chinese, Greek, Basque, Palestinian, German-Russian communities
6. **Condiment containment** is a systematic blind spot — condiments are invisible inside their region. Seed-to-longform loops work: use existing condiments to find longform pieces that name parallels, extract new entries, repeat until returns diminish.
7. **City-level depth is the next frontier.** State-level listicles surface 1-2 items per city, but food-dense cities have 5-10+. JBF-winning items (STL hot salami) can hide behind more "clickbait-friendly" items (toasted ravioli, provel). The fix: city-specific food guides, not state-level lists.
8. **Seed-to-discovery validated in Phase 6.** Known gaps used as search seeds landed in city food guides that named unknown items. Red Hot Riplets (STL) and Shrimp DeJonghe (Chicago) were NOT in any gap analysis — they surfaced because gap seeds landed us in city-specific articles. Yield: 11 entries from 3 cities, 2 genuine unknowns. Diaspora queries ("moved from STL miss food") produced 8-10 item lists per thread. Chicago's Wikipedia "Culture of Chicago" food section alone named 15+ hyper-local items.
9. **JBF America's Classics ≠ hyper-local dish.** JBF Classics honor *restaurants*, not *dishes*. Many winners are beloved single institutions (Johnny's Cafe, Shady Glen, Fry Bread House) that don't represent broader regional food traditions. The award is a useful lead generator but needs a containment filter: does the *dish* exist beyond one restaurant? If no, it's an institution, not a regional food.

## PHASE 6: CITY-LEVEL DEEP DIVES

### The Problem
State-level sweeps produced ~168 entries but treated each city as having 1-2 signature items. In reality, cities with deep immigrant/working-class food cultures have **clusters** — and the state listicle format only surfaces the most "weird" or photogenic item, burying the rest. The STL hot salami proves this: JBF America's Classics winner, Food & Wine "Best in Missouri," yet invisible in every state-level listicle because toasted ravioli and provel are weirder.

### The Fix: Seed-to-Discovery Strategy
Known gaps from JBF/Food & Wine lists are NOT the deliverable — they're **search seeds**. Each seed gets us into city-specific articles and diaspora threads that name multiple items. The items we DON'T already know about are the real yield.

**Per-city search battery:**
1. `"[known gap item]" "[city] food"` → lands in city food guides listing 10-15 items
2. `"moved from [city]" "miss" food "can't find"` → diaspora lists 5-10 items at once
3. `"[city] food" James Beard` → JBF as containment signal
4. `"[city] food" "underrated" OR "overlooked" OR "nobody talks about"` → second tier
5. Cross-reference all results against our doc → anything in 3+ sources but NOT in doc = new entry

**Expected yield per city**: 2-5 UNKNOWN items on top of the known gaps. The known gaps are entry points; the unknowns are the payoff.

### Selection Criteria for Target Cities
Cities qualify if they have **3+ existing entries** (indicating dense food culture) OR are known food cities where we have suspiciously few entries.
