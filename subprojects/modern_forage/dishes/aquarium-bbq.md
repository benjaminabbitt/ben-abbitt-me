# Aquarium-Smoked Rib Tips & Hot Links

## What it is

Chicago-style barbecue is the only regional American barbecue defined less by *what* is smoked than by *how* and *where*. The signature apparatus is the **aquarium smoker**: a steel-framed, two-chamber pit with a wood firebox in the lower chamber and a meat chamber above, fronted by sliding glass panels (the "aquarium"). Smoke vents straight up an enclosed metal chimney to the outdoors; the cook stays inside. The meat staples cooked in it are **rib tips** (the cartilaginous trim left after butchering St. Louis-style ribs — cheap, fatty, perfect for fast direct heat) and **hot links** (a Chicago-Black-food-culture style sausage with cayenne, paprika, garlic). Both go into styrofoam containers, get doused in a tomato-based sweet-and-tangy sauce, and ride a stack of white bread used to soak up the rest.

## Origin

- The aquarium smoker emerged in 1954 in Chicago as Black families who'd migrated from the Mississippi Delta and Arkansas tried to keep cooking Southern-style barbecue inside Chicago city buildings during winter, against fire codes, and within zoning that didn't allow open outdoor pits.
- **Myles Lemons** opened **Lem's Bar-B-Q** at 311 E 75th St the same year — 1954. James Lemons (Myles's brother / cousin in the family network) is the most-cited oral historian of the form (Southern Foodways Alliance, 2008 oral history).
- The design solves several constraints simultaneously: the enclosed firebox keeps a wood fire legal indoors; the glass front lets the pitmaster watch the meat without opening a hot box; the chimney concentrates draft for fast cooking (1-3 hours vs. the all-day Southern open-pit timeline); the sliding glass doubles as a window-display advertisement.
- No single inventor is documented — the form appears to have emerged across several South Side BBQ joints in the early-to-mid 1950s. Lem's is the most-cited canonical version.

## Why it's contained

Most American regional barbecue (Memphis dry rub, Texas post oak, Carolina vinegar, KC tomato) travels because the *technique* is portable: any pit anywhere can copy it. Chicago-style barbecue does not travel because the *apparatus* is non-portable: building an aquarium smoker requires a specific permit, ventilation, fabricator, and pitmaster training. The smokers are typically built into the storefront and stay there for the life of the building. Of the canonical Chicago BBQ joints, all are in Black neighborhoods on the South and West sides; none have franchised; none have an out-of-state location.

The dish is recognizable from the rest of the country (rib tips and hot links exist elsewhere), but the smoking method and the storefront-aquarium presentation do not. Saveur, Eater, Chicago Magazine, Smithsonian, and the James Beard Foundation have all framed Chicago-style barbecue as a regional style as distinct as Memphis or Texas.
