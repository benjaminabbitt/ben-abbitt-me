## Summary

This document classifies every inferential step in [The Antisocial Contract](/posts/the-antisocial-contract/) by the degree to which the cited literature supports the claim. The purpose is transparency: the reader should know where the argument rests on firm empirical ground and where it extends beyond what the evidence strictly establishes.

**Overall assessment:** The core causal chain (categorization → fusion → sacralization → backfire → analytical redirection → intractable conflict) is well-supported at each individual step. The weakest links are (a) the claim that these individual steps form a *self-reinforcing feedback loop*, which has not been tested as a system, and (b) the generalization from conflict-negotiation contexts to routine democratic politics. No step is unsupported; several require additional evidence to move from reasonable inference to established finding.

| Classification | Count | Description |

| **DIRECT** | 5 | The cited literature explicitly supports this claim |
| **SUPPORTED INFERENCE** | 7 | The cited literature supports the components; the connection is reasonable but not explicitly made by the cited authors |
| **LEAP** | 3 | The argument goes beyond what any cited source claims; needs additional support or flagging |

## Method

Every inferential step in the argument chain was extracted and classified:
- **DIRECT** — The cited literature explicitly supports this claim
- **SUPPORTED INFERENCE** — The cited literature supports the components; the connection is reasonable but not explicitly made by the authors
- **LEAP** — The argument goes beyond what any cited source claims; needs additional support or flagging

## THE CORE CHAIN

### Step 2: Religion produces unusually potent group identity
- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- Ysseldyk et al. (2010) argues this explicitly. However, the claim that religious identity is *qualitatively different* from other identity types (not just quantitatively stronger) is an interpretation. Ysseldyk argues for potency based on perceived immutability and cosmic sanctioning, but doesn't empirically test whether religious identity produces *more* bias than equivalently strong secular identities.
- **What would strengthen it:** A study directly comparing in-group bias intensity across identity types (religious vs. national vs. political vs. ethnic) controlling for identification strength.
- **Status in current document:** The primary argument has been revised to treat religion as a classical example with extensive history rather than as categorically different from other identity types.

### Step 5: Analytical capacity is not absent but redirected toward rationalization
- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- The Expressive Rationality Model (Bayrak et al., 2025) shows that higher CRT scores in religious individuals correlate with *stronger* identity-consistent reasoning. The interpretation that this constitutes "analytical capacity redirected toward rationalization" is the document's framing, not Bayrak et al.'s. They describe it as identity-consistent reasoning moderated by religiosity. The word "rationalization" (with its pejorative connotation of motivated reasoning) is an editorial choice.
- **What would strengthen it:** Evidence that high-CRT religious individuals produce *qualitatively different* defenses of their positions (more sophisticated arguments, more effective counter-argumentation) compared to low-CRT religious individuals — demonstrating that the cognitive ability is actively deployed in service of the sacred commitment.
- **Status in current document:** The primary argument uses the term "defense" rather than "rationalization" in most instances and cites Bayrak et al. directly.

### Step 6: The neurological substrate maps onto executive function
- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- Zhong et al. (2018) shows PFC lesions → religious experience → magical ideation. The inference that "the PFC regions implicated are the same regions responsible for overriding intuitive responses" is factually correct as a neuroscience claim, but the paper frames this as "PFC damage opens people to religious experiences," not as "executive function suppression by religion."
- **CAUSAL DIRECTION NOTE:** The paper shows PFC suppression *enables* religion, not that religion *suppresses* the PFC. These are different claims. The primary argument should not reverse this causal arrow.
- **What would strengthen it:** Neuroimaging studies showing reduced PFC activation *during* sacred-value reasoning in neurologically intact individuals. The Berns et al. (2012) and Hamid et al. (2019) studies provide this, showing diminished deliberative-region activation during sacred-value processing.
- **Status in current document:** The primary argument cites Hamid et al. (2019) for the fMRI evidence of diminished dlPFC/IFG activity during sacred-value reasoning, which addresses this gap.

## THE FEEDBACK LOOP

### Warfare → suffering → religious recruitment → identity hardening → sacralization → intractable conflict → more warfare

- **Classification: LEAP (as a closed loop)**
- Individual arrows are supported:
  - Warfare → increased religiosity: Shayo & Zussman (2022). DIRECT.
  - Religiosity → sacralization: Sheikh et al. (2012) shows religious ritual increases sacred-value holding. DIRECT.
  - Sacralization → intractable conflict: Ginges et al. (2007). DIRECT.
  - Intractable conflict → more warfare: trivially true.
- **The leap:** Closing the loop and calling it *self-reinforcing* implies a positive feedback dynamic that has not been empirically tested as a system. No study tracks the full cycle. Each individual arrow is supported, but the claim that the system is *self-reinforcing* (rather than self-limiting or oscillating) is an inference.
- **What would strengthen it:** A longitudinal study tracking religiosity, sacralization, and conflict intensity over multiple cycles in a single population. The closest is the RELAC data showing religiously framed conflicts last longer — consistent with self-reinforcement but not proof of the full loop.

## THE GENERALITY CLAIM

### "Any exclusionary group identity can trigger the same mechanism; religion is a historically effective delivery system"

- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- The minimal group paradigm shows that *any* categorization produces bias. This is DIRECT.
- The claim that secular ideologies *sacralize comparably* to religious ones is supported by Atran & Ginges (2012), who explicitly state that "otherwise mundane sociopolitical preferences may become sacred values." However, the claim that they sacralize with the *same cognitive architecture* (same backfire effects, same neural signatures) is now supported by Berns et al. (2012), which found identical neural activation patterns for religious and secular sacred values.
- **What would strengthen it:** Experimental replication of the Ginges et al. (2007) paradigm with explicitly secular sacred values (national sovereignty, free speech absolutism, gun rights) to test whether the backfire effect operates with comparable intensity.

## EXCLUSIVISM AS STRUCTURAL FEATURE

### Proselytizing mandate → exclusivism → competitive dominance (selection effect)

- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE with elements of LEAP**
- The observation that proselytizing religions (Christianity, Islam) dominate world population is a fact.
- The cultural evolutionary theory (Norenzayan, 2013; *Scientific Reports* 2022) supports the mechanism: prosocial religions outcompete through cooperation → fecundity → expansion.
- **The leap:** The primary argument attributes the competitive advantage specifically to *exclusivism* rather than to *prosociality* (Norenzayan's framing) or *costly signaling* (Sosis's framing). These traits correlate with exclusivism but are not identical to it.
- **What would strengthen it:** Within-religion comparison showing that exclusivist denominations grow faster than pluralistic ones (e.g., Southern Baptists vs. Episcopalians, Salafi Islam vs. Sufi Islam, Orthodox Judaism vs. Reform Judaism). The Iannaccone (1994), Thomas & Olson (2010), and Pew (2015, 2021) data now cited in the primary argument provide substantial support for this.

## PROSELYTIZING RELIGIONS INDUCE DEFENSIVE EXCLUSIVISM IN OTHERS

- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- The Religious Freedom Institute analysis documents this for Hindu/Buddhist communities responding to Christian proselytism. The European populism literature (Cremer, 2023; Haynes, 2020) documents secular European societies developing "Christian civilizationist" identities in response to Islam.
- **The leap:** Calling this "induced defensiveness" implies a causal mechanism (proselytizing → defensive exclusivism) rather than merely a correlation. The causal claim is plausible but not rigorously established through experimental or longitudinal methods.
- **What would strengthen it:** A natural experiment or longitudinal study showing that communities exposed to proselytizing activity develop exclusivist attitudes *after* exposure, controlling for pre-existing attitudes.

## THE DEMOCRATIC TRAP

### Sacred-value constituencies constrain leaders who are privately more rational

- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- Atran's leader interviews (2007) directly show leaders are more instrumentally rational than publics regarding symbolic concessions. This is DIRECT for the conflict-negotiation context.
- **The leap:** Generalizing from conflict negotiation to democratic governance broadly — claiming that the same mechanism constrains representatives in electoral democracies generally, not just in peace negotiations specifically.
- **What would strengthen it:** Studies of elected representatives' private vs. public positions on sacralized issues showing systematic divergence between private instrumental assessments and public sacred-value signaling. The January 6th text message evidence and the Cheney/Kinzinger electoral outcomes provide suggestive contemporary evidence.

## COLD WAR AS SECULAR SACRED-VALUE CONFLICT

### Deterrence theory as analytical redirection

- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- The framing of Cold War strategic analysis as "analytical capacity redirected toward rationalization" is an interpretive choice. Deterrence theorists would argue they were performing genuine instrumental analysis under extreme uncertainty.
- **Complicating factor:** Deterrence *worked* — the Cold War ended without nuclear exchange. If the analytical output (MAD) produced a successful outcome, the framework's prediction of catastrophic failure from analytical redirection is not straightforwardly confirmed.
- **Where the framework does apply:** Vietnam (Pentagon Papers showing private instrumental assessment contradicting public sacred-value commitment) and the bomber/missile gap intelligence failures (mirror-imaging as identity projection) are stronger examples of analytical redirection.

## MAGA AS EXCLUSIONARY IDENTITY

### Material incentives backfire for MAGA supporters

- **Classification: SUPPORTED INFERENCE**
- Koenig et al. (2025) shows MAGA participation is about status/honor, not economic self-interest. The ACA/Obamacare/Romneycare evidence now cited in the primary argument provides strong support for label-driven evaluation overriding policy content.
- **What would strengthen it:** Direct experimental replication of the Ginges et al. (2007) backfire paradigm with MAGA-sacralized values (election integrity, immigration).

### Identity fusion in MAGA supporters

- **Classification: DIRECT (now supported)**
- Moniz & Swann (2025), Martel et al. (2024), and Kunst et al. (2019) directly measure identity fusion with Trump using Swann's validated instruments and find it predicts support for political violence, authoritarianism, and misinformation acceptance.
